# Tech Team Tools — User Personas

## Overview

Tech Team Tools is a suite of apps for engineering managers and their teams — covering 1:1s, retrospectives, risk assessments, and team capability benchmarking. Four personas interact with the platform across different levels of the organisation.

---

## Persona 1 — The Engineering Manager (Primary User)

**Who they are**
An engineering manager leading a team of 4–12 developers. Previously an IC who stepped into management 1–3 years ago. Comfortable with technical tools but new to structured people management. Often managing 1:1s and retros through ad hoc notes or Google Docs.

**What they want**
- A structured place to prepare for and document 1:1 conversations with each direct report
- A simple retrospective tool their team will actually use each sprint
- To understand how their team is tracking against best practices (the Accelerate 24 capabilities)
- To show their own manager that the team is improving over time

**Key frustrations**
- 1:1 notes live in random documents, action items are forgotten, history is lost when team members leave
- Retros feel like they produce the same action items every sprint — nothing sticks
- No way to benchmark the team's maturity against any external reference
- Spends too much time preparing for quarterly check-ins with leadership

**Success looks like**
Every 1:1 has a clear agenda, historic context, and tracked action items. The team's retrospective actions have an owner and a due date. A monthly capability assessment shows concrete improvement over the quarter.

---

## Persona 2 — The Individual Contributor (Team Member)

**Who they are**
A developer, QA engineer, or designer on a product team. Age 22–40. Uses Jira, Slack, GitHub daily. Skeptical of management tools that produce no visible benefit for them.

**What they want**
- 1:1 preparation to feel useful — not just a performance review-shaped conversation
- Retros where their feedback is heard and actioned, not ignored after the session
- Visibility into their own development and goals set in previous 1:1s

**Key frustrations**
- Past retro actions disappeared into a Confluence page nobody visited
- 1:1s often feel like status updates rather than career development conversations
- No continuity — a new manager meant losing all history

**Success looks like**
They walk into a 1:1 with their talking points already noted, leave with clear commitments from the manager, and can track whether those commitments were followed up the following fortnight.

---

## Persona 3 — The CTO / VP Engineering (Executive Sponsor)

**Who they are**
A technical leader responsible for multiple teams. Age 35–55. Focused on delivery, team health, and hiring/retention. Will approve tooling purchases if they see org-level reporting value.

**What they want**
- Org-wide view of team health across all their engineering managers
- Early signals of team dysfunction (low retro participation, repeating issues, stalled capability scores)
- Data to present to the board or CEO about engineering investment and improvement
- A tool that helps managers become better managers — reducing escalations to the CTO

**Key frustrations**
- No aggregated view of what is happening across teams — relies on anecdote and escalations
- SWOT and risk assessments happen in PowerPoint once a year, if at all
- Cannot tell which teams are high-performing and which are struggling until a crisis

**Success looks like**
A monthly dashboard showing retro health, 1:1 consistency, and capability trends across all teams. Able to identify which teams need coaching before problems surface in delivery metrics.

---

## Persona 4 — The HR Business Partner / People & Culture Manager

**Who they are**
An HR professional embedded in or partnered with a technology team. Non-technical but comfortable with data and reporting. Focused on engagement, retention, and performance management.

**What they want**
- Evidence that managers are having consistent, quality conversations with their reports
- Data to support annual performance cycles and promotion decisions
- Tools that align to the company's leadership framework without a full HRIS integration

**Key frustrations**
- Engineering managers do not document 1:1s consistently — no evidence trail when disputes arise
- Engagement survey data is available once a year; real-time signals would be far more useful
- Has to manually chase managers to complete performance documentation

**Success looks like**
A clear record of 1:1 cadence, agreed goals, and retrospective participation that can be pulled into performance reviews without bespoke effort from the manager or HR.
